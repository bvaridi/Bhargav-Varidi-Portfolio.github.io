The Turning Point

How a pharmacist ended up building EHR workflows

The shift didn't happen in a classroom — it happened across several primary care centers in India, during my PharmD research project. I was collecting patient data the only way those settings allowed: on paper.

What I found was unsettling. Records were incomplete, entries were missing, and some data was simply gone — lost to time, damage, or never written down consistently in the first place.

I remember realizing that a single misplaced folder could erase a patient's history.

That fragility is what first pulled me toward EHR systems — not as a technology trend, but as a real fix for a problem I'd watched cause harm. It's the same instinct I bring to every workflow I touch today, Epic or otherwise: data isn't just data, it's someone's care record.

Usability Evaluation of OpenEMR

Academic Project — Team of 4 ("Semantics Squad") Jan 2024 – May 2024
Problem

OpenEMR, a widely used open-source EHR, needed a structured usability evaluation — clinicians widely report dissatisfaction with EHR interfaces despite their value, but most issues are never formally documented.

Approach
  • Served as team lead — coordinated task assignments, tracked project progress, and proofread/submitted deliverables
  • Built and ran a real-world patient case study (intake, diagnosis, prescribing, follow-up) to stress-test the system end-to-end
  • Co-developed task evaluations across 4 simulated patients to assess core workflows: patient creation, chart review, encounters, and data entry
Outcome

Identified concrete usability failures — scattered vitals placement, unintuitive save-button positioning, missing facility-type options, and no way to remove erroneous medication entries — with direct implications for data accuracy and patient safety.

Analysis of Lung Cancer Risk Factors to Predict Severity

Academic Project — Team of 6 2024
Problem

A 1,000-patient lung cancer dataset (23 risk factors spanning behaviors, health conditions, environment, and symptoms) needed statistical analysis to identify which factors most strongly predict disease severity, then translate those patterns into predictive models.

Approach
  • Co-led data cleaning, exploratory analysis, and visualization (correlation heatmaps, Spearman significance testing, forest plots)
  • Helped build and evaluate three predictive models — linear regression, decision tree, and k-nearest neighbors — comparing accuracy and overfitting risk across each
  • Authored and edited the project proposal, methodology, and results documentation
Outcome

Identified statistically significant correlations between all 23 factors and severity, and critically evaluated model overfitting risk — recognizing that near-perfect accuracy on even random factor subsets signaled a dataset validity issue rather than genuine predictive power.

Python scikit-learn Pandas Seaborn
